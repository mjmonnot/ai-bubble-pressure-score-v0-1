# 2. The Economics of Bubbles

## 2.1 Classic Bubble Definition  
The defining elements of a speculative bubble include:

1. **Displacement** (technological shock)  
2. **Credit expansion**  
3. **Euphoria**  
4. **Financial distress**  
5. **Revulsion and collapse**  

(Kindleberger & Aliber, 2011)

AI represents a *displacement event* with characteristics similar to railroads, electrification, radios, dot-coms, and crypto assetsâ€”each triggering investment cycles decoupled from steady-state fundamentals (Perez, 2002).

# 5. Infrastructure, Bottlenecks, and Real-Economy Constraints

AI development is unusually constrained by **physical infrastructure**:

- power generation  
- transmission  
- cooling  
- datacenter construction  
- semiconductor manufacturing  

Economic history shows that bubbles often end when **real-world bottlenecks collide with speculative expectations** (e.g., railroad bubbles of the 1840s; electrification; telecom bandwidth in 2000).

The **Infrastructure pillar** captures this friction region.

# 6. Capex Cycles and Overproduction

Overinvestment cycles are well studied:

- **Pigou cycles**  
- **Cobweb models** (Kaldor, 1934)  
- **Bullwhip effect dynamics**  
- **Boom-and-bust capex oscillations** (Jorgenson, 1963)

AI follows this pattern:

1. Demand shock (LLMs, inference, GPUs)  
2. Massive capex buildouts  
3. Supply glut or infrastructure saturation  
4. Price collapse or equilibrium correction  

The **Capex Supply pillar** maps these dynamics using PNFI, UNXANO, and planned industry-level indicators.
